Most planners are designed by people who color-code their sock drawer. This one is not that. It is built for the version of you who just wants to make it through the week without three things falling through the cracks.

I have bought, downloaded, and abandoned more planner systems than I want to admit. Most of them died the same way: too many sections, too many categories, too much guilt by Wednesday when half the boxes were still empty.

## What Makes a Reset Planner Different

A reset planner is not trying to optimize your whole life. It exists to do one thing well: give your week a shape before the week gives itself a shape for you, which is usually chaos.

There are four sections, and that is intentional. Anything beyond four sections starts feeling like homework, and homework is the fastest way to make someone quit a system in under a week.

### Section One: The Three That Matter

Not a ten-item to-do list. Three things. The three that, if they got done and nothing else did, would still make the week feel worth something.

This forces an actual decision about priority instead of just transcribing every task floating around your brain onto paper and calling it a plan.

### Section Two: The Maybe List

Everything else lives here. Lower stakes, lower pressure, get to it if the week allows. Half the stress of a normal to-do list comes from treating every item as equally urgent when most of them are not.

### Section Three: One Thing for You

A single line, every week, for something that is just for you. A book chapter, a bath, ten minutes outside with no phone. Most planners forget this section exists. This one does not.

### Section Four: The Honest Check-In

A short space at the end of the week to note what actually happened versus what you planned. Not for guilt. For information. Some weeks the plan and the reality will not match, and that tells you something useful about the week, not about your worth.

## Why This Works Better Than a Bullet Journal

A full bullet journal system is beautiful and, for a lot of people, completely unsustainable. It requires consistency that most weeks simply do not allow.

This planner takes about four minutes to fill out on a Sunday night or Monday morning. Four minutes is sustainable. An hour of habit tracking and mood logging is not. Not for most actual weeks with actual jobs, actual kids, and actual exhaustion in them.

## How to Use It

Print a few weeks at once so it is ready before you need it. Fill it out at the same time each week if you can. Right before coffee or right after the kids are down, whatever slot is most likely to actually happen.

And if a week gets away from you and the page stays blank, skip it and start fresh the following week. One missed week does not break the system. It just makes you human, which was never in question anyway.
